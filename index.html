How machine learning (and quantum tech) could predict the stock market

I wanted to know if a computer that checks every possibility at once could actually read the market better than a normal one. So I built this to compare classical and quantum machine learning on the same stock data, and see what quantum computing really changes right now, and what it doesn't. Yet.

02. Quantum foundations

The building blocks, before we get to finance

Before applying any of this to stock data, here is the underlying physics and math this project rests on.

Qb

Qubits

The quantum version of a bit. Instead of being strictly 0 or 1, a qubit is described by a combination of both. Its exact state only decides when measured.

Sp

Superposition

The property that lets a qubit hold a weighted blend of 0 and 1 at the same time, letting a circuit represent many scenarios at once instead of one at a time.

En

Entanglement

When two qubits become correlated so strongly that measuring one instantly tells you something about the other, no matter how they are processed afterward.

Gt

Quantum gates and circuits

Gates are the operations that rotate and entangle qubits, the quantum equivalent of AND/OR logic gates. Chaining gates together builds a quantum circuit, the quantum version of a program.

Bs

The Bloch sphere

A way of visualizing a single qubit's state as a point on a sphere. The north and south poles are 0 and 1, and everywhere else represents a superposition between them.

Sd

Schmidt decomposition

A math theorem that lets you break down the state of two entangled qubits into simpler, paired up pieces. It's how physicists formally measure how entangled a system actually is.

Reading the sphere

The arrow is a single qubit's state. Straight up means a definite 0, straight down means a definite 1. Anywhere else on the surface is a valid superposition, with the angle determining the exact mix.

In this project, gate operations are rotations of this arrow around the sphere. Training a variational circuit means learning the right sequence of rotations for each qubit.

03. Applying it to finance

Where these ideas actually get used

The foundational concepts above show up directly in how this project encodes and processes stock data.

Fm

Quantum feature maps

Classical stock data (price, volume, momentum) gets encoded into qubit rotation angles. This turns numbers into something a quantum circuit can act on.

Vc

Variational quantum circuits

A circuit with tunable knobs, trained the way a classical neural network is. It's adjusted step by step until it predicts price direction more accurately.

<
Qk

Quantum kernels

Instead of training a circuit directly, this measures how similar two days of market data are in a much richer, higher dimensional space than classical methods reach.

Dc

Decoherence and the NISQ era

Today's qubits lose their quantum state within microseconds due to noise. This is the honest ceiling on current hardware, and why results here are modest, not magic.

Sh

Shor's algorithm

Not used directly in this project, but the landmark result that proves quantum computers can outpace classical ones. It factors large numbers exponentially faster, which is also why it threatens today's encryption.

Tq

Topological quantum field theory

A more advanced, forward looking direction. Topological qubits are designed to resist noise structurally, which could eventually solve the decoherence problem above.

04. How it actually works

From stock chart to quantum circuit

Four steps turn raw price history into something a quantum computer can process, and eventually, a prediction.

01

Encode the data

Historical price, volume, and momentum indicators are normalized and mapped onto qubit rotation angles. This is the quantum feature map in action.

02

Build the circuit

A variational quantum circuit is assembled from layers of rotation and entangling gates, each with adjustable parameters. This is the qubit equivalent of a neural network's weights.

03

Measure and compare

The circuit is measured, collapsing superposition into a classical output, a predicted up or down. This is compared against the actual historical result.

04

Train, hybrid style

A classical optimizer adjusts the circuit's parameters based on the error. This loop between classical and quantum computation is how nearly all real QML runs today.

05. The results

Did quantum win?

Not yet, and that's the more honest, more interesting answer.

Reality check: today's quantum hardware is small and noisy (the NISQ era: noisy, intermediate scale quantum). In this project, the quantum model didn't outperform the classical one on prediction accuracy. What it does show is the mechanism: how superposition, entanglement, and quantum feature maps change the shape of the search itself, and why researchers expect an advantage to emerge as hardware improves.
